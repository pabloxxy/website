Simulating a Brownian excursion from a Brownian bridge

Let's consider \(B_t \) a standard Brownian motion in \([0,1]\). We can think of the process \[B_1-tB_t \text{ for } t\in[0,1],\] which is called a Brownian bridge, as a Brownian motion conditioned to be \(1\) at time \(t=1\). As one can imagine, if we are able to simulate Brownian motion (which we are), then a Brownian bridge is simple to simulate.

What happens if we further want to condition a Brownian motion to stay positive in \([0,1]\)?

This new process, called a Brownian excursion is not so simple to simulate. Here we show one possible way, sometimes called the Vervaat Transform, to transform a Brownian bridge into a Brownian excursion.

Given a Brownian bridge, find its (almost surely unique) minimum. Then cyclically shift to the minimum and we get a Brownian excursion. Equivalently, we can swap the parts before and after the minimum and then translate vertically. The details of why this transformation is indeed a Brownian excursion can be found in Vervaat's article: A relation between Brownian bridge and Brownian excursion (1979)
